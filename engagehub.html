Employee experience platform · 25,000 employees

Helping 25,000 people feel more connected at work — and giving HR a way to see if it was working.

EngageHub pulls work, life, wellbeing, events and feedback into one place for a mostly-remote, global workforce. Most of the work was making it easy to take part — and making those everyday actions tell HR something useful. Accessibility shaped decisions from the first screen; it wasn't a pass at the end.

01 · The problem worth solving

A 25,000-person company wanted people more engaged — and had no real way to tell if they were.

Much of the workforce was remote. Engagement ran on manual effort, and no one had the data to fix it.

A lot of the company worked remotely, and they were the ones most out of the loop. HR ran engagement and events by hand, without much sense of who was taking part or what people actually thought, and the tools were scattered. There was no single place where this happened and could be seen — so engagement was mostly guesswork. Four groups needed different things: HR wanted data and a way to run events; employees wanted to feel connected and take part without friction; managers wanted a read on their teams; and remote staff just wanted to stop feeling like an afterthought.

04 · Product decisions

What I chose, and what I traded away.

Three of the calls that shaped it — what I was weighing, what I picked, and what I gave up.

DECISION 01

Show the week at a glance — don't make people scroll to find out what's on

Problem
Participation depends on discoverability. In testing, people wouldn't scroll to find events, and a time-strip view went unused.
Options weighed
  • Vertical scrolling list — familiar, but the week is hidden below the fold.
  • Time-strip / agenda — precise, but users ignored it in testing.
  • A seven-day week grid with every event and its Join / Reminder in the first view.
Choice & why
The week grid — because the fastest way to raise participation is to make participation visible without a single scroll. This was the change I made after usability testing.
Trade-off I accepted
A denser layout — managed with compact event cards and category colour + label so it stays scannable.
DECISION 02

Categories carry colour and label and icon — never colour alone

Problem
Five engagement pillars — Work, Life, Enrich, Wow & Pride, Connect & Community — run through the calendar, hub, favourites and gallery. Colour-coding alone excludes colour-blind and screen-reader users, and WCAG/ADA was a hard requirement across 25,000 people.
Options weighed
  • Colour-only tags — compact and common, but fail WCAG 1.4.1 (use of colour).
  • Colour + text label.
  • Colour + text + icon, applied consistently on every surface.
Choice & why
Every category is named everywhere, with colour as reinforcement — not the only signal. Inclusive by construction, and easier for everyone to learn.
Trade-off I accepted
Labels take more room than a colour dot — a fair price for a taxonomy 25,000 people, including AT users, can actually read.
DECISION 03

Make taking part safe and low-friction — and let participation become the signal

Problem
Two problems at once: employees hesitate to post in a work social space, and HR had no continuous engagement data.
Options weighed
  • Open, unmoderated feed — lively, but risky in a work context.
  • No social layer — safe, but misses the connection that was the whole point.
  • Moderated posting with a visible approval note, plus one-tap mood and event RSVPs.
Choice & why
Lower the cost of showing up — a clear “admin approves” note builds trust — and design the light actions (mood, Join, ratings) so that participation itself becomes the measurement HR was missing.
Trade-off I accepted
Moderation adds latency — softened by auto-publishing on approval and setting the expectation up front.

05 · Accessibility, by design

Built in from the first screen — not audited in at the end.

In a 25,000-person organisation, accessibility isn't a finishing pass. Here's where it shaped the design.

Colour is never alone · WCAG 1.4.1

Every engagement category shows a word beside its colour, on every surface — so the taxonomy works without perceiving hue.

Mood, in words as well as faces

The check-in labels each emoji — Excited, Happy, Cool, Boring, Frustrated, Angry — so the meaning doesn't depend on reading an emoji.

Forms that explain themselves · WCAG 3.3.2

Contact and feedback use visible labels and clear required markers, not placeholder-only fields that vanish as you type.

Contrast tuned to context · WCAG 1.4.3

A dark theme for browsing, and a light, high-contrast surface for reading-and-writing tasks like rating an event.

08 · If I ran it again

What I'd do differently.

Instrument earlier

Wire participation metrics into the flows from day one, so impact is provable per feature, not just at the platform level.

Tokenise the taxonomy

The colour + label category system should be design tokens from the start, so it stays consistent as the platform grows.

Test with AT users first

Put the dense calendar in front of assistive-technology and lowest-tenure users earliest — that's where accessibility is really proven.
