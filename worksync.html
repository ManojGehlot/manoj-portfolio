Internal workforce & payroll operations tool

Every cell in this grid is money that's wrong until someone makes it right.

WorkSync is the control surface where an ops team creates projects, tags employees, runs rosters, and processes arrears and allowances. A mistake here isn't a bad pixel — it's an underpaid employee and an audit trail that doesn't hold. This is the story of designing for that stake.

01 · The problem worth solving

Correct pay was running on manual effort and memory.

Four roles depended on the same brittle process. When it broke, it broke as wrong pay, delayed approvals, and reports no one could trust.

Managers created projects and tagged employees; line managers had no window into what those managers had done; team leads chased approvals; HR tried to pull accurate payroll and shift data in real time and couldn't. Arrears and allowance adjustments — money owed, corrected after the fact — lived partly in spreadsheets and manual calculation. The business problem underneath every user complaint was the same: the operation had no single, trustworthy source for pay-affecting actions, so accuracy depended on people not making mistakes under deadline.

03 · Principles I actually designed against

Four rules, each a tie-breaker for a money workflow.

01

Correctness over speed of entry

In a flow that feeds pay, prevent the error even if it costs a step.

02

Make status visible

No one should ever wonder “is this approved, applied, or wrong?”

03

Traceability by default

Every pay-affecting action carries who, when, and an approval it can be checked against.

04

One job per role

Give each of the four roles their own surface instead of one crowded view.

04 · Product decisions

The part a reviewer reads for: what I chose, and what I gave up.

Three decisions, each shown as problem → options → choice → trade-off, anchored to the screen that resulted.

DECISION 01

Tag employees through a guided flow with a commit gate — not one dense form

Problem
Tagging is where wrong data enters and later corrupts pay. Users were making errors 20% of the time.
Options weighed
  • One power-form — fastest for experts, but errors commit silently.
  • Inline tagging inside the table — quick, but no review moment.
  • Guided steps: Select project → Select employees → Confirm & submit.
Choice & why
The guided flow, because the explicit Confirm & Submit step turns a silent error into a caught one before it reaches payroll.
Trade-off I accepted
One extra step for high-volume users — mitigated by pre-filling and keeping each step light.
DECISION 02

Represent arrears & allowances as a status-colored matrix — not a list of records

Problem
Allowance and arrears are per-employee, per-day, with many states. A record list hides whether anyone's pay is wrong until you open each one.
Options weighed
  • Record table — familiar, but you can't see a month at a glance.
  • Per-employee detail pages — accurate, but buries the pattern in clicks.
  • Employee × day matrix with a color legend + a calculate/process action.
Choice & why
The matrix, because it turns “is anyone's pay wrong this month?” into a visual scan instead of a query — directly serving the 75% of HR who couldn't verify in real time.
Trade-off I accepted
Density has an onboarding cost — mitigated with a persistent legend and per-cell detail on demand.
DECISION 03

Build role-shaped surfaces with an approval trail — not one universal view

Problem
Four roles with four different jobs shared one process — and line managers (70%) were blind to what managers had done.
Options weighed
  • One dashboard for everyone — cheapest to build, noisiest to use.
  • Role-tailored surfaces + an approval/notification trail across them.
Choice & why
Role-shaped views, because the approval trail is what gives line managers the visibility they asked for while keeping each role focused on its own job.
Trade-off I accepted
More screens and permission logic — a large share of the 50+ screens — in exchange for far less noise per role.
05 · The screen this project lives or dies on

Designing a grid where every cell is a payment.

If the allowance matrix is unreadable, WorkSync fails — this is where correctness has to become visible.

Scan before read

Status is carried by color, so a full month reads as a pattern first. The eye finds the wrong cell before it parses the number.

Legend as contract

A fixed legend keeps every color meaning one thing across the whole surface — the vocabulary never shifts under the user.

Calculate, then commit

Processing is a deliberate action at the edge of the grid, not a side effect of editing — money moves only on intent.

06 · Outcomes

What the design was built to change — stated honestly.

User outcome

Verifying a month of allowances went from opening records one by one to a single colored scan.

User outcome

The commit gate gives a place to catch tagging mistakes before they reach pay. Target: reduce the 20% tagging-error baseline.

Business outcome

The approval trail gave line managers the visibility 70% said was missing — and gave every pay action a record.

Business outcome

One system replaced a fragmented, partly-manual process — the single platform 85% said they wanted.

The honest part

Post-launch metrics weren't instrumented in this project. Validation was prototype testing with 8–10 users across roles, with strong qualitative response and feature requests folded into a next phase. I've framed impact as targets against real baselines, not as claimed wins — and instrumenting those baselines is the first thing I'd change (below).

07 · If I ran it again

What eight weeks taught me about designing for money.

Instrument day one

Wire the 20% error rate and 12-minute creation time as live metrics, so impact is proof, not intent.

Test density earliest

Put the allowance matrix in front of the lowest-tenure HR users first — that's where its readability is really tested.

Cut the artifacts that didn't decide

Two personas and four empathy maps were more than the decisions needed. I'd keep only the ones that changed a choice.
